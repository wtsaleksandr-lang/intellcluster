Most decision-making advice is about process — run the meeting this way, consider these stakeholders, document the outcome. Almost none of it is about the mechanical skill of *actually weighting criteria*. That skip is weird, because weighted criteria are the single most leveraged element in any structured decision.

Weights are what separate a decision model that reflects your priorities from one that's window dressing on a pre-made conclusion. Teams that get weights right make dramatically sharper decisions. Teams that get weights wrong produce elaborate scoring spreadsheets that confirm whatever they wanted to do anyway.

Here's the practical skill, made explicit.

## What weights actually do

In a weighted decision model, you list criteria (e.g., price, reliability, ease of use) and assign each a percentage weight that sums to 100. Each option gets scored per criterion, the scores get multiplied by the weights, and the products sum into a final score.

The math is boring. The weights are the decision.

Consider: three criteria — price (20%), reliability (60%), ease of use (20%) — produce a very different ranking than the same three criteria at (60%, 20%, 20%). Same options, same per-criterion scores, different winner. The weights *are* the strategic stance you're taking.

Which means: **if the weights are wrong, the output is wrong, regardless of how carefully you scored.**

## Three bad weight-setting patterns

A catalog of how teams go wrong.

### 1. The "everything is important" flat-weight

Give every criterion equal weight because "they all matter." Symptom: five criteria each at 20%. Output: no discrimination — options cluster around the mean final score. The model has no teeth.

This pattern reflects unwillingness to make the priority call. If everything is equally important, nothing is prioritized, and the decision isn't actually being made structurally — it's being made by whichever option happens to score highest across the flat surface.

Fix: force a prioritization. If you can't pick which criterion is 2× more important than another, you're not ready to decide.

### 2. The back-solved weights

Start with the option you prefer. Adjust weights until that option wins. Produce a scoring matrix that looks rigorous.

This is the most insidious pattern because it's often unconscious. You *want* the decision to be clean, so you shift weights until the math matches your intuition. The matrix feels like analysis. It's actually rationalization.

Fix: write the weights **before** scoring. Or better: write the weights before you've even narrowed the option set. The weights should reflect what matters to the business, not which option you happen to prefer.

### 3. The too-many-criteria blow-up

Criteria list of 15+ items. Each with a tiny weight. The team spent a week negotiating the list; nobody is willing to drop items.

Symptom: scoring takes 2 hours per option because there are 15 cells to fill. Output: the weights are so small individually (5–10% each) that scoring noise dominates the signal. A difference of 0.5 points in any single cell can flip the winner.

Fix: forcibly consolidate. 5–9 criteria is the useful range. If you have 15, cluster them. Several "nice to have" items become a single "nice-to-haves" criterion with 10% weight. Keep the top criteria explicit and aggregate the rest.

## The weight-setting protocol

Here's the protocol we recommend. It takes 20–30 minutes the first few times and gets fast.

### Step 1: Generate the raw list

Ask the team: "If we were evaluating this decision, what criteria would matter?" Free-list, no judgment. Typical output: 10–20 items.

### Step 2: Cluster

Group similar criteria. "Ease of use" and "learning curve" are probably one criterion. "Price" and "total cost of ownership" probably are. Consolidate into 6–10 items.

### Step 3: Rank order

Ask the team: "What's the single most important criterion?" Pick one. Then: "What's the second?" Pick the next. Continue through the list. This produces an ordered list without yet assigning numbers.

Ranking is cognitively easier than numeric weighting. By the end of this step, you have structure to hang numbers on.

### Step 4: Assign percentages to the ordered list

Here's the specific protocol that works. Give the #1 criterion its weight first — typical range 20–30% for the most important item. Then the #2 — typically 70–90% of #1's weight. Then work down.

Check sum after each assignment. When the bottom criteria run out of room, you'll discover that you over-weighted the top. Adjust proportionally downward on the top criteria until the sum is 100.

The shape you're aiming for: top criterion 20–30%, criteria 2–4 at 15–20%, criteria 5–7 at 5–10%, criteria 8–9 at 2–5%.

### Step 5: Debate the weights before scoring

This is the most important step. Now that the weights are proposed, debate them as a group. Does anyone disagree that price is 20%? That team familiarity is 5%?

The debate about the weights *is* the strategic discussion. Teams that debate weights thoroughly rarely debate the final output, because the weights embody the agreement. Teams that skip weight debates often get stuck debating the output — re-litigating the weights under the guise of challenging the scoring.

### Step 6: Freeze and score

Once weights are committed, lock them. Score each option against each criterion. If the scoring process reveals a missing criterion, don't add it mid-scoring — capture it for the next version of the model, but complete scoring against the frozen weights.

## What the weights should reflect

A common mistake: teams set weights that reflect **what's nice to have** rather than **what separates options**. These are different.

Example: when evaluating CRMs, "must work on mobile" is probably a 0-weight criterion — all real CRMs work on mobile, so it doesn't discriminate. "Sophistication of email automation" might be 15% — this genuinely varies across CRMs and matters to your use case.

The rule: weight by **discriminating power**. A criterion that's identical across all options deserves no weight, even if the criterion is critically important. A criterion that varies meaningfully across options — even a secondary one — deserves more weight.

This is counterintuitive. Teams often protest: "but reliability is the most important thing!" If all three options are equally reliable, reliability is a threshold (must clear it), not a discriminator (doesn't weight in). Separate those concepts: must-haves vs discriminators, and only weight the discriminators.

## The pre-mortem move

Once weights are set, run a pre-mortem: "What option would win if we optimized *purely* for the top-weighted criterion?" If the answer is the option you secretly wanted to win, check yourself — the weights may be back-solved.

Conversely, "What option would win if the bottom-weighted criterion was weighted like the top?" If the answer is an option you find embarrassing, the bottom-weighted criterion is probably doing useful work — keep it in the model.

These two checks catch the most common weight-setting biases without requiring paranoid self-reflection.

## Common weight ranges

For specific decision types, rough weight patterns we've seen work:

**Vendor selection (SaaS):**
- Must-have features: 25%
- Total cost (year 1 + projected): 20%
- Integration fit: 15%
- Ease of admin: 10%
- Support SLA: 10%
- Exit cost: 10%
- Renewal flexibility: 5%
- Contract terms: 5%

**Hiring (senior individual contributor):**
- Core skill quality: 25–30%
- Collaboration / communication: 15–20%
- Ownership / initiative: 10–15%
- Culture add: 5–10%
- Ramp time: 10%
- Growth trajectory: 10%

**Product feature prioritization:**
- Customer value (demand-weighted): 30%
- Effort / cost: 25% (negative-weighted)
- Strategic alignment with OKR: 20%
- Risk (technical + business): 15% (negative-weighted)
- Team fit / excitement: 10%

**Technical architecture choice:**
- Performance at projected scale: 20%
- Operational burden: 20%
- Team familiarity: 15%
- Integration with existing stack: 15%
- Cost at projected scale: 10%
- Community / ecosystem maturity: 10%
- Exit cost: 10%

These are starting points. Adjust for your specific context.

## The weight-drift problem

Over a quarter of decisions, weights drift unless deliberately maintained. What started as "price is 20%" creeps to "price is 30%" by decision 15, because price keeps coming up as a constraint and the team informally weights it higher.

The fix: explicit "weight review" sessions. Once per quarter, examine the weighted criteria templates you've been using. Are the weights still right for the current business context? If the company's priorities have shifted (e.g., cost discipline is now paramount), bump the cost weight explicitly. If growth is paramount, bump the criteria that discriminate on growth potential.

Making weight drift deliberate rather than accidental maintains alignment between stated priorities and actual decision-making.

## Teaching the skill

Weighted-criteria thinking is a learnable skill. In our experience, it takes:

- **Session 1 (30 minutes):** The conceptual framework. Why weights matter, the three bad patterns, the protocol above.
- **Session 2 (60 minutes):** Hands-on — each participant runs a real decision they're facing through the framework, gets feedback on the weights.
- **Weeks 3–8:** Practice on real decisions with light coaching. Usually 4–5 decisions before the skill is internalized.

After that, weighted-criteria thinking becomes automatic. Teams stop arguing about options and start arguing about weights, which is the argument that actually matters. Meetings get faster. Decisions get sharper. Documentation gets cleaner.

The skill transfers across decision types. Once someone is good at weighting vendor decisions, they're good at weighting hiring decisions, product decisions, and architectural decisions — the underlying skill is the same.

## The broader point

Weighted criteria are not fancy process jargon. They're the concrete mechanism by which strategic priorities translate into specific choices. Teams that skip them are making strategic decisions implicitly, by the priors of whoever happens to be loudest in the room.

Invest 30 minutes in the weight-setting skill for your team. Adopt the protocol above. Run the next five non-trivial decisions through a weighted model — either in a spreadsheet or in [Phronesis](/phronesis) — and grade the decision quality against the unweighted alternatives.

You'll find that the weights become the most important artifact of your decision process. Not the scores. Not the final ranking. The weights — because they encode what matters, and everything else follows from there.
